My Project

My project was inspired by my love for cooking and food and also the need in the US for food, because 37.8 million people are currently suffering from food insecurity in the US alone. I wanted to try and have an impact in lowering the number but also making sure people are fed and happy. And to do that I volunteered at the SF Marin FoodBank and Hamotzi (I will talk about those later). I also feel that food is something that everyone needs. Read more below about the two programs that I volunteered at, and the impact they are making for people in the bay area.

SF-Marin Food Bank

Mission

The SF Marin FoodBank's mission is to end hunger in San Francisco and Marin.

"Before the pandemic, one in five neighbors was at risk of hunger. Now, we know the need is even greater. We address hunger head-on - from our food pantry network and home-delivered groceries to CalFresh (food stamp) enrollment. Every week, over 53,000 households count on us for food assistance. 60% of what we distribute to them is fresh fruit and vegetables."

My Experience

My experience with the food bank has always been filled with fun, music, and of course food. There usually are some 80s or 90s tunes playing as you put into a box cans, milk, cereal, meat, or any other things. The way they make it most efficient is they have it in an assembly line style. You get assigned to a job, or you can pick it. It could either be putting in some canned fruit, vegetables, meat, or milk, powder milk, cereal. There are many more jobs other than handling the canned and prepackaged goods. You can man the tape machine, it is a machine that tapes the bottom and top. If you ever go there, know that the bottom is not sealed, so be careful. If you want to do a harder job, you can lift boxes. By doing this you put it on the pallet that they then wrap and deliver to elders across SF and Marin. They always remind you that every box that is made, is handed out to one person. So if you made 600 boxes, you would have given food to 600 people. Specifically older people. I usually go at least once a month, so I usually have the same thing that happens. But there was one time that we worked with raw chicken that had frozen from Chick Fil A. I also recommend bringing your gloves. They do have disposable gloves there, but it is better to bring your own so you don't waste gloves. At the end of the shift which is around 2-3 hours (it goes by fast) the manager of the shift gives you the numbers of boxes you packed, the weight of all the boxes in all, and then they lead you out with star wars music, or any other marching music. This is also a great opportunity to get volunteer hours for school.

Hamotzi

Mission

Hamotzi's mission is to feed people in need and need food. They deliver to Compass Family Services, and Women's Place. This program started during the High Holy Days in 1993. Since then they have served over 150,000 meals to shelters in San Francisco. They also have another program inside of Hamotzi for new families called Chesed. Chesed is targeted for people who have just had a baby and are starting a new family.

My Experience

My experience there was really good, it was full of nice and caring people. When I was there I was chopping veggies, and doing prep work. Cleaning tables and packing up the food. It is a very good environment and you can always step away and take a break. My personal favorite thing to do is cutting veggies because you can get an arm workout and also talk to people and meet them. Another thing that they do is you have to wear a hair net or a baseball cap to keep hair out of food, and wear gloves when handling food. It starts around 9 o'clock, and ends around 12:30.
